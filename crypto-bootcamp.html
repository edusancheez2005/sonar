CH 01What money actually is

Before crypto, before trading, before any of this matters — you need a working definition of money. Most people skip this and then never quite understand why bitcoin exists. Don't skip it.

Money is three things at once:

  1. A medium of exchange — I give you this, you give me a sandwich.
  2. A unit of account — we can both quote the price of the sandwich in the same number.
  3. A store of value — if I save it today, I can spend it next month for roughly the same sandwich.

Anything that does all three reasonably well is money. Gold did it for 5,000 years. The US dollar does it today, backed by the US government's ability to tax and to enforce contracts. Crypto attempts to do it backed by math and incentives instead of governments.

All money is a trust-substitute. The question is just: trust in what?

Why this matters for trading

When you trade crypto, you're not just betting on a coin going up. You're betting on whether more people will trust this thing tomorrow than today. That's it. Every technical chart, every "alpha signal", every whale tracker is a downstream proxy for that one question: is conviction in this asset rising or falling?

Checkpoint 1

Which of the three functions of money is hardest for a brand-new crypto token to satisfy on day one?

Medium of exchange
Unit of account

Why?A new token can technically be exchanged and quoted immediately. But "store of value" requires that next month it's worth roughly what it's worth today — and a token with no track record has zero evidence of that. This is why 99% of new tokens fail: they never earn the store-of-value status.

CH 02Blockchains in 10 minutes

A blockchain is a shared database that nobody owns. That's it. Everything else is plumbing.

Imagine a Google Sheet where every row is a transaction ("Alice sent Bob 5 coins"). Now imagine that instead of one server holding the sheet, 10,000 computers around the world each hold an identical copy, and they agree on the next row using a voting rule. No single computer can cheat, because the other 9,999 would notice and reject the bad row.

Key insight
Every transaction is public. When Alice sends Bob 5 BTC, the whole world sees it. This is the foundation of on-chain analytics — and of your whale-tracking engine. If a wallet holding $50M moves to an exchange, anyone watching can see it. That's a signal.
Deep dive: how mining / staking actually picks the next block

Proof of Work (Bitcoin): Computers race to solve a meaningless math puzzle. First one to solve it earns the right to write the next block and gets paid in BTC ("block reward"). The puzzle is hard on purpose — it makes attacking the network ruinously expensive.

Proof of Stake (Ethereum, Solana, ...): Instead of burning electricity, validators lock up ("stake") their own coins as collateral. The protocol randomly picks a validator weighted by stake. If they cheat, the protocol burns their collateral ("slashing"). Cheaper and greener, same security property.

CH 02·5Consensus deep dive — who actually "solves" a block

This is the chapter that turns "blockchain" from buzzword into machinery. Skip nothing here.

The problem the network has to solve

Imagine ten thousand computers around the world, all wanting to agree on the exact same list of transactions, in the exact same order, with no boss. Without a central authority, who decides which transactions go into the next block, and in what order?

If you just let everyone broadcast their own version, you'd get chaos — different computers would see different orderings, conflicting transactions, and an attacker could spam the network with fake "official" lists.

So the network needs a rule for picking one computer to propose the next block, and that rule has to be:

  1. Random enough that no one can predict or game it.
  2. Expensive enough that an attacker can't just spin up fake identities.

There are two big designs in use today.

Bitcoin — Proof of Work ("mining")

The rule: whoever first solves a hard math puzzle gets to write the next block.

The puzzle is deliberately useless. It's literally: "find a number N such that when you hash (block contents + N), the result starts with, say, 19 zeros." There's no clever way to solve it. You just guess trillions of numbers per second until you get lucky.

Hash("block data" + 1)         = 0xa3f8b2...  ❌ doesn't start with 19 zeros
Hash("block data" + 2)         = 0xc91e4f...  ❌
Hash("block data" + 3)         = 0x7d2a0c...  ❌
...
Hash("block data" + 8,472,931) = 0x000000000000000000091e... ✅ winner!

Why it works:

  • It costs real-world electricity to guess. A modern Bitcoin miner burns ~$5–10 of electricity per second of guessing.
  • The first one to find a valid N broadcasts the block. Every other node verifies it instantly (one hash, microseconds) and moves on.
  • To cheat, you'd need more compute than the rest of the network combined ("51% attack") — currently tens of billions of dollars per hour.
Why every 10 minutes?
The protocol adjusts the difficulty every 2,016 blocks (~2 weeks). If miners are solving blocks too fast (more miners joined), the puzzle gets harder. Too slow? Easier. The target is always "average one block every 10 minutes." It's not magic — it's a feedback loop. Ethereum's target is ~12 seconds with a different mechanism.

Who is the miner? Today, mostly industrial-scale operations: warehouses in Texas, Kazakhstan, Iceland — wherever electricity is cheapest. Each "miner" is a rack of specialised chips (ASICs) doing nothing but guessing hashes. They get paid two ways:

  • Block reward — the protocol mints new BTC and gives it to whoever solves the block (currently 3.125 BTC ≈ $190k per block).
  • Transaction fees — every transaction the miner includes pays them a tip.

Ethereum — Proof of Stake ("staking")

Ethereum used to use Proof of Work like Bitcoin, but switched in September 2022 ("The Merge"). The new rule: the protocol randomly picks a validator, weighted by how much ETH they've locked up.

To become a validator, you put 32 ETH (~$80k) into a special contract as collateral. Every ~12 seconds, the protocol picks one validator at random (weighted by stake) and says "you produce the next block." Other validators vote on whether the block is valid.

  • To attack the network, you'd need 51% of all staked ETH — currently $50B+.
  • If you cheat (sign two conflicting blocks, go offline, etc.), the protocol burns your collateral ("slashing"). You lose your 32 ETH.
  • Behave → earn ~3-4% yield. Misbehave → lose everything.

Same goal as Bitcoin (make attacking ruinous) but using financial stake instead of physical electricity.

What "constantly updated" means — gossip propagation

When a new block is produced:

  1. The producing node broadcasts it to its peers.
  2. Each peer verifies it (signatures, hashes, balances).
  3. If valid, they add it to their copy and relay to their peers.
  4. In ~5 seconds, the whole world has it.

Checkpoint 2·5

An attacker wants to rewrite last week's Bitcoin history. To do it, they need to:

Hack a single mining pool
Bribe Satoshi
Re-mine every block from then to now, faster than the entire honest network combined
Get 51% of full nodes to agree
Why?Each block contains the hash of the previous one. Changing one historical block invalidates every subsequent block's hash. To overwrite history you'd need to redo all that Proof of Work faster than the honest miners are producing new blocks on the real chain — economically impossible at Bitcoin's current hashrate.

CH 02·75Smart contracts & how Aave actually works

You've heard the word "smart contract" a thousand times. Here's what it actually is, and how the most-used DeFi protocol in the world (Aave) is built out of one.

The one-paragraph version

A smart contract is a small program that lives on a blockchain. You deploy it once; afterwards anyone in the world can call its functions, and the blockchain itself guarantees the code runs exactly as written and that the results (balances, ownership, state) are recorded permanently. There's no company hosting it, no admin who can pause it, no database you can ask to roll back a transaction. The code is the institution.

The vending-machine analogy

Think of a vending machine:

  • You put in $2.
  • The machine, by its mechanical design, must give you a Coke.
  • There's no clerk to argue with, no "let me check with my manager".
  • The rules are visible and enforced by the physical mechanism.

A smart contract is the same idea, but the "machine" is code running on thousands of computers simultaneously, and the "Coke" is anything digital — tokens, NFTs, a loan, an insurance payout, a vote.

What makes it different from a normal program

Normal programSmart contract
Runs on one server you controlRuns on every node in the network
You can patch it, pause it, delete itOnce deployed, immutable (unless you built an upgrade hatch)
Stores data in your databaseStores data on-chain — public forever
You pay for the serverThe caller pays gas to execute it
Bug = embarrassingBug = $600M gone in one transaction (Poly Network, 2021)

What smart contracts enable — the whole DeFi universe

  • DEXes (Uniswap, Jupiter) — automated trading without an exchange company
  • Lending (Aave, Compound) — deposit collateral, borrow against it, all enforced by code
  • Stablecoins (DAI, USDC) — tokens whose supply rules are coded
  • NFTs — a contract that tracks "address X owns token #1234"
  • DAOs — voting + treasury management as code
  • Perpetual futures (dYdX, GMX) — the whole derivatives stack

Worked example — how Aave works

Aave is a lending market — like a bank, but as a smart contract. Two roles: lenders (deposit) and borrowers (borrow). No human approves anything. No credit check.

The core idea

A big shared pool of USDC sits in a smart contract. Anyone can deposit USDC to earn interest, or borrow USDC by paying interest. But you can't sue an anonymous Ethereum address — so how do they stop borrowers running away with the money?

The answer: over-collateralisation
To borrow $1,000 of USDC, you must first lock up something worth more — say $1,500 of ETH — as collateral. The contract holds it hostage. If you don't pay back, the contract sells your ETH to recover the loan.

Why would anyone do this? Because they:

  • Don't want to sell their ETH (tax reasons, conviction it'll go up), but
  • Need cash now (to buy something, to lever up, to short, etc.).

They keep ETH exposure and get cash. The cost is interest + the risk of liquidation.

The full flow

STEP 1 — LENDER side
  Alice deposits 10,000 USDC into Aave's USDC pool.
  Contract gives Alice 10,000 aUSDC tokens (the "receipt").
  Alice's aUSDC balance grows continuously — that's her interest.

STEP 2 — BORROWER side
  Bob deposits 5 ETH (~$15,000) as collateral.
  Aave lets him borrow up to 75% of that value → $11,250 max.
  Bob borrows 8,000 USDC. He now has the cash + still owns the ETH.

STEP 3 — INTEREST
  Bob pays, say, 5% APR on his 8,000 USDC loan.
  That interest flows into the pool, increasing the value of every aUSDC.
  Alice's 10,000 aUSDC slowly grows to 10,300 aUSDC over a year.

STEP 4 — REPAYMENT (happy path)
  Bob earns money, repays 8,000 USDC + interest.
  Contract releases his 5 ETH. Done.

STEP 5 — LIQUIDATION (sad path)
  ETH crashes $3,000 → $2,200. Collateral now worth $11,000,
  Bob owes $8,000 — that's 73% LTV, dangerously close to the 75% limit.
  At 80% LTV, the contract opens Bob's position to anyone in the world:
  "anyone who repays Bob's 8,000 USDC gets his ETH at a 5% discount."
  A liquidator bot spots this in milliseconds, repays the debt, takes
  Bob's ETH worth $10,450 → instant $550 profit.
  Alice's pool is made whole. Bob is left with whatever ETH was leftover.

Where the interest rates come from

No Aave employee sets rates. The contract has a formula:

$$\text{borrow rate} = f(\text{utilisation})$$

where utilisation = how much of the pool is currently borrowed. If 80% of the USDC is lent out, rates rise (incentivises more lenders, discourages more borrowers). If only 10% is lent out, rates fall. Pure supply and demand, enforced by math, updated every block.

Why this is wild

  • Aave holds ~$15 billion in deposits across chains.
  • It has been running since 2020. Has never been shut down. Has no CEO. Has no headquarters.
  • It's ~2,000 lines of Solidity at a fixed Ethereum address.
  • All of it is public. You can watch every deposit, every loan, every liquidation in real time on Etherscan. (Your whale tracker watches exactly this kind of activity.)
The catch (always read this)
  • Bugs are catastrophic. A typo in a contract holding $200M can drain the whole pool. No undo, no support ticket.
  • Immutability cuts both ways. Great for trustlessness; terrible when you find a bug and can't fix it.
  • Gas is real money. Every line that runs costs a fee to the network. Inefficient contracts are unusable.
  • "Decentralised" is often a lie. Many contracts have an onlyOwner function letting one key-holder upgrade or pause everything. Always check.

Checkpoint 2·75

Why does Aave require borrowers to post collateral worth more than what they borrow?

Regulation forces them to
To make extra profit on the spread
Because anonymous on-chain borrowers can't be sued — over-collateralisation makes default impossible to profit from
Because ETH is volatile
Why?The whole protocol is trustless. There's no court, no collections agency, no identity check. The only way to guarantee lenders get repaid is to make sure the collateral is always worth more than the loan — and to auto-liquidate the instant that ratio gets dangerous. The buffer (e.g. 75% LTV cap, 80% liquidation threshold) protects against volatility.

CH 04The order book — the heart of price

If you only understand one mechanism in trading, make it this one. The order book is where supply meets demand, in real time, in public.

Two kinds of orders exist:

  • Limit order: "I want to buy 1 BTC at $60,000 or lower." You sit and wait until someone agrees. This adds liquidity to the book.
  • Market order: "I want to buy 1 BTC now, at whatever the best price is." You eat whatever's available. This removes liquidity.

Sellers stack above, buyers stack below. The gap between the best bid and best ask is the spread — the market's friction.

When you place a market buy for 2 BTC, you eat:

  1. 0.8 BTC at $60,100 → $48,080
  2. 1.2 BTC at $60,150 → $72,180
  3. Total: 2 BTC for $120,260, average price $60,130

Your average fill ($60,130) is worse than the best ask ($60,100). That difference is called slippage. The bigger your order vs. book depth, the worse it gets. This is why large trades are split into pieces ("algorithmic execution").

The price you see is a lie
When CoinGecko says "BTC = $60,100", it means "the best ask is $60,100 for some tiny size". The real price you pay depends on how much you're buying and how thick the book is. Pros never quote one number — they quote a price at a size.

CH 05Spot vs. derivatives

So far we've talked about spot: you buy the coin, you own the coin. Simple.

Derivatives are contracts whose value derives from the spot price, but you never touch the underlying coin. The two that matter:

1. Futures

"I agree to buy 1 BTC at $65,000 on December 30." If BTC is above $65k on Dec 30, you profit; below, you lose. Used for hedging and leveraged speculation.

2. Perpetual futures ("perps")

Crypto invented this. A perp is a future with no expiry. To keep its price tethered to the spot price, the exchange charges a funding rate every 8 hours:

Funding rate — the most underrated signal
  • If perp price > spot → too many longs → longs pay shorts (positive funding)
  • If perp price < spot → too many shorts → shorts pay longs (negative funding)
When funding gets extreme (say > 0.05% per 8 hours, i.e. ~50% annualised), it tells you the market is dangerously one-sided. This is Family D in your engine. Crowded longs get liquidated on small dips — so the contrarian short trade has positive expectancy.

The funding mechanism is a self-correcting loop. When it gets stuck at extremes, that's the alpha.

Leverage — the trader killer

Derivatives let you put down 10% margin to control 100% of a position ("10× leverage"). If the price moves 10% against you, you're wiped out ("liquidated"). Most retail accounts that touch 50× or 100× leverage die within weeks. Pros rarely use more than 2–3×.

The math of leverage
At 10× leverage, a 10% adverse move = 100% loss. At 20× leverage, a 5% move = 100% loss. Crypto routinely moves 5% in an hour. Do the math.

CH 06Liquidity, slippage, market makers

Liquidity = how much you can trade without moving the price. It's the single most important number after price itself.

Who provides this liquidity? Market makers. They post both bids and asks simultaneously, hoping to collect the spread on every round trip. They're the casino. You're the gambler.

sequenceDiagram
  participant R as Retail trader

Every time you round-trip, the market maker pockets the spread. Multiply by millions of trades per day.

Sophisticated market makers (Jump, Wintermute, GSR, Cumberland) run statistical models to:

  • Widen the spread when volatility spikes (they don't want to get run over by news)
  • Skew quotes when their inventory gets lopsided
  • Pull quotes entirely when something feels off (flash crashes happen because everyone pulls at once)

CH 07How retail loses, how pros win

This is the hardest chapter to internalise. Retail traders lose money structurally, not because they're dumb. The system is designed to extract from them.

The four retail death modes

  1. Buying tops, selling bottoms. Retail enters when price has already moved (FOMO from news/Twitter) and exits when it's already crashed (capitulation). Pros are the counter-party on both sides.
  2. Over-leverage. Discussed above. The exchange wants you to use leverage — they earn fees and liquidate you.
  3. Trading too often. Every trade pays the spread + fee. Even with 55% win rate, churning 50× per day will bleed you to zero.
  4. No risk management. Doubling down on losers ("averaging into a loss"), no stop losses, betting too much per position.

How pros win

  1. They have an edge they can measure. Win rate × avg win > loss rate × avg loss. Anything else is gambling.
  2. They size positions by edge. Big edge = big bet. Tiny edge = tiny bet. Never bet the farm.
  3. They diversify across uncorrelated strategies. Five strategies with 1.0 Sharpe ratio each combine to ~2.2 Sharpe if uncorrelated. Diversification is the only free lunch in finance.
  4. They are paranoid about overfitting. A strategy that worked in backtest but fails live is the default outcome. Pros assume their model is wrong and design to survive being wrong.

The market regime changed

CH 09What is a "signal"?

A signal is a specific, repeatable, measurable condition that predicts future return better than chance. That's the whole definition.

It is not:

  • A vibe ("BTC feels overbought")
  • A narrative ("ETH will moon because of the upgrade")
  • A chart pattern with no statistical backing

To qualify as a signal, you need three things:

  1. Trigger: a precise condition. "Funding rate > 0.05%/8h". Not "funding looks high".
  2. Direction: long or short.
  3. Edge: measurable positive expected return on past data, robust to overfitting.

How signals are scored (and miscored)

Most signal engines do something like this: each Family outputs a number in [-1, +1] (negative = short, positive = long, magnitude = conviction). You combine them into a composite score, then bucket the score into discrete labels (STRONG BUY / BUY / HOLD / SELL / STRONG SELL).

This is fine — if you actually measured that each Family is predictive. The trap is to combine 5 Families that sound good but were never individually validated. You get a beautiful number that's negatively correlated with reality. (Sound familiar? It's literally what happened to your engine — ρ = −0.141.)

The professional rule
Validate each Family in isolation before combining. Each Family must clear a statistical bar (e.g. Spearman ρ > 0.05 with future return at p < 0.01) on out-of-sample data. Then — and only then — you combine. The combination should improve Sharpe, not just sound better.

CH 10Backtesting (and how it lies to you)

Backtesting = running your strategy on historical data to see how it would have performed. It's the most important — and most dangerous — tool in quant trading.

The seven deadly sins of backtesting

SinWhat it isHow to detect
Look-ahead biasYour strategy "knows" something it couldn't have known in real time (e.g. uses tomorrow's price to decide today's trade)Audit your code for any reference to future-indexed data
Survivorship biasYou only test on tokens that exist today, ignoring the 1000s that diedUse a point-in-time universe
OverfittingYou tuned parameters until the curve looked great. It won't generalise.Out-of-sample test. K-fold CV. The smaller the gap between train and test performance, the safer.
Ignoring fees + slippageStrategy makes 10 bps per trade. Round-trip cost is 15 bps. Net loss.Always model realistic execution.
Small sample"My strategy hit 8 out of 10 trades" — meaninglessNeed hundreds of independent trades minimum.

Data snoopingYou tested 200 variations and reported the best oneBonferroni-correct your p-values, or use a holdout you only touch once.

The metrics that actually matter

Forget annualised return. Pros judge a strategy on:

Sharpe ratio = (mean return − risk free) / std deviation of returns
Risk-adjusted return. Sharpe < 1.0 = retail. 1.0–2.0 = decent. > 2.0 = institutional-grade. > 3.0 = rare and likely overfit.
Maximum drawdown
The worst peak-to-trough loss. A strategy with 50% annual return and 60% max drawdown is unholdable — you'd panic out before recovery.
Calmar ratio = annual return / max drawdown
Cleaner than Sharpe for tail-risk-aware traders.
Hit rate
% of trades that win. Often misleading — a 30% hit rate strategy can be highly profitable if the wins are big.
Profit factor = gross wins / gross losses
Combines hit rate and avg win/loss. > 1.5 is good.

The math underneath Sharpe:

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{E[R_p - R_f]}{\sigma_p} \cdot \sqrt{N}$$

where $R_p$ is your strategy's return, $R_f$ is the risk-free rate, $\sigma_p$ is the standard deviation of returns, and $N$ is the number of periods per year (252 for daily, 8760 for hourly).

CH 11Risk: the only thing that matters

You'll hear pros say "alpha is overrated, risk is everything". They mean it literally. A strategy with mediocre returns and great risk control will outlive a brilliant strategy with sloppy risk every time.

Position sizing — the Kelly criterion

Given an edge, how much of your bankroll should you bet? The math has a clean answer (Kelly, 1956):

$$f^* = \frac{p \cdot b - (1-p)}{b}$$

where $p$ = probability of winning, $b$ = ratio of average win to average loss, and $f^*$ = fraction of bankroll to bet.

Example: a strategy wins 55% of the time, with avg win = avg loss (b = 1). Then $f^* = 0.55 \cdot 1 - 0.45 = 0.10$ — bet 10% of the bankroll per trade. Most pros use a fractional Kelly (often 0.25× or 0.5×) because the full formula assumes you know your edge perfectly. You don't.

Bet too big and you die
Even a strategy with positive expected value will blow up if you bet too much per trade. The volatility of compounding kills you long before the expected value rescues you. This is the difference between "expectation positive" and "growth optimal".

Stop losses, take profits, and asymmetric payoffs

Every trade should have:

  • An entry rule (signal fires)
  • An exit rule for being right (take profit at X)
  • An exit rule for being wrong (stop loss at Y)
  • A time stop (close after N hours regardless)

Aim for a reward:risk ratio of at least 2:1. If your average winner is 2% and average loser is 1%, you only need a 35% hit rate to be profitable.

Portfolio-level risk

Even with great per-trade risk, your overall portfolio can blow up if:

  1. Positions are correlated (all crypto crashes together)
  2. You concentrate too much in one trade ("conviction" is the most dangerous word in finance)
  3. You can't exit ("liquidity risk") — common in alt-coins during crashes

Checkpoint 11

Your strategy has 60% hit rate, 1:1 reward/risk. Full Kelly says bet 20%. You bet 20% per trade. After 50 trades you've lost 80% of your capital despite the strategy "working". Why?

The strategy stopped working
Variance — you hit a losing streak that compounded brutally at full Kelly
Fees
Bad luck
Why?Full Kelly maximises expected log wealth, but with massive drawdowns. A run of 10 losses in a row at 20% per trade leaves you with (0.8)^10 ≈ 11% of starting capital. This is why fractional Kelly (typically 0.25×) is industry standard — slightly slower compounding, dramatically smaller drawdowns.

CH 13Glossary — the words you'll hear constantly

Ask
The lowest price at which someone is willing to sell.
Bid
The highest price at which someone is willing to buy.
Spread
Ask − Bid. The cost of round-tripping a position.
Liquidity
How much you can trade without moving the price.
Slippage
The difference between expected fill price and actual fill price.
Order book
The live ledger of all open buy and sell limit orders on an exchange.
Market order
Buy/sell immediately at the best available price.
Limit order
Buy/sell only at price X or better; sits in the book until filled or cancelled.
Spot
Trading the actual asset for immediate delivery.
Futures
Contract to buy/sell at a fixed price on a future date.
Perpetual / Perp
A futures contract with no expiry, kept tethered to spot via funding rate.
Funding rate
Periodic payment between longs and shorts on a perp to keep its price near spot.
Open Interest (OI)
Total notional value of open derivative contracts. Rising OI + rising price = strong trend; rising OI + falling price = capitulation incoming.
Long
Betting price will go up.
Short
Betting price will go down.
Leverage
Borrowed money to amplify position size. 10× = $1 of margin controls $10 of asset.
Liquidation
Forced closure of a leveraged position when collateral runs out.
Margin
The collateral you post against a leveraged position.
Stop loss
An order that closes a position when price hits a threshold to cap losses.
Whale
An entity (person/fund) holding enough of an asset to move price by trading.
Smart money
Wallets with a documented track record of profitable trades; worth following.
Exchange inflow/outflow
Coins moving onto / off of exchanges. Inflow usually = selling intent.
On-chain
Anything happening directly on the blockchain (vs. on a CEX's internal database).
Off-chain
Inside an exchange's books; not visible publicly.
Stablecoin
Token pegged to a fiat currency (USDT, USDC, DAI). The bridge between crypto and dollars.
Alt / Altcoin
Any non-Bitcoin cryptocurrency.
Market cap
Token price × circulating supply.
FDV (Fully Diluted Valuation)
Token price × total supply if all tokens were unlocked. Often much higher than market cap.
Sharpe ratio
Risk-adjusted return measure. >1 decent, >2 excellent.
Alpha
Return in excess of a benchmark. The thing all of this is trying to generate.
Beta
Sensitivity to a benchmark. Beta = 1 means moves with BTC 1:1.
Drawdown
Peak-to-trough loss in capital. Max drawdown is the worst one.
Backtest
Simulating a strategy on historical data.
Walk-forward / OOS
Testing on data the model has never seen. The only test that matters.
Overfitting
A model that fits past noise rather than real signal. Will fail live.
Look-ahead bias
Accidentally using future information in a backtest. The most common bug.
Edge
The statistical advantage over random.
Hit rate
Fraction of trades that are profitable.
Kelly criterion
Optimal bet sizing formula given known edge. Always use a fraction of it.
Sentiment
Aggregated bullishness/bearishness, typically derived from social or news.
HODL
Buy and never sell. A real strategy with surprisingly good long-term Sharpe in crypto.
DeFi
Decentralised finance — lending, trading, derivatives on-chain via smart contracts.
Gas
Transaction fee paid to the blockchain to process your transaction.
Custody
Who controls the private keys. Self-custody = you do. Custodial = an exchange does.
